The finding

Funded fintechs build product far faster than they build the ability to sell it.

Across 318 venture-backed fintechs, the open engineering roles outnumber the open commercial roles nearly 5 to 1, and only 5% are actively hiring a go-to-market leader. This is where they are investing at the margin, the direction of the next dollar of headcount, not a snapshot of the team they already have. The capital lands, the product ships, the team keeps scaling engineering, and the commercial engine is the piece left unbuilt. Growth quietly stays founder-dependent, and the next raise leans on revenue the company cannot yet make repeatable.

This is counted, not claimed, from 122 observable hiring signals. What follows is where the gap is worst, what the founders themselves say about it, and the specific places where a company's stated strategy and its observable reality have quietly come apart.

In their own words

We did not only count the signals. We listened to 53 founders.

Transcribed founder podcast appearances are the closest thing a private company has to an earnings call, the moment a founder stops marketing and starts reasoning. Six themes recur. If you run one of these companies, the point is simple: you are not the only one.

Trust and incumbency are the real barrier, not the product

“The only two other providers are 150-year-old banks. We're a new, almost unknown entity, so trust is hard to build. It's working one by one with each RIA.”

Srikanth Narayan, CEO, Cache

The buying trigger is the economics, not the mission

“I don't even know if we had climate in the deck, honestly. It didn't matter, because this is where the industry is moving for purely economic reasons.”

Samir Pendse, CEO, Coral

We're creating a category that has no budget line yet

“We create demand in a category with no budget line, by selling the future investment process, not a product.”

Eric Chang, CEO, Claira

The buyer we sell to is not the buyer who funds it

“Finance has often been a blocker to new revenue. Product says 'let's sell this a new way to drive revenue', and finance says 'I can't fit that in my billing and rev-rec system.'”

Founder, Tabs

Product works; a repeatable engine doesn't exist yet

“To start seeing people who are actually paying us money get results is the best thing ever. One firm moved over $500k in new assets.”

Co-founder, Wealth Reach

Where strategy and reality diverge

The most useful finding is not what companies are doing. It is where what they say and what they do have come apart.

Reading the founder's narrative against the hiring, the website, the pricing, and the public peer surfaces contradictions the company often cannot see from the inside. Four patterns dominate. Every example below is specific and evidence-based.

Serving every buyer, focused on none

The most common contradiction in the universe. A single product and one motion pointed at three or four structurally different buyers, each with a different decision-maker, deal size, and sales cycle. Focus is the cheapest growth lever at this stage, and almost no one is using it.

AlgebrikThree solution pages (credit unions, banks, auto lenders) but the integration stack reveals only TWO real motions (CU cores Corelation/Jack Henry + auto rails RouteOne/Dealertrack/Carfax). 'Banks' is positioned but not plumbed.
Farsight AIGives all four buyers (IB/PE/wealth/hedge) co-equal billing, picks none, hires 4:1 product-to-GTM, no pricing, no logos, no packaging model. One engine for four buying motions.
FintarySegments by insurance LINE (Life/Health/P&C/Carriers) but collapses three opposed economic buyers (producer wants payout, broker wants margin, carrier wants reconciliation) into one 'agency leadership' persona and one ROI story; even the carrier page leads with 'producer experience'.
OsyteSeed stage with ~3-5 customers, sells one 'multi-asset workbench' message to SFOs, MFOs/OCIOs, AND institutional managers, three structurally different buyers (principal vs platform buyer vs procurement committee) with incompatible deal sizes and processes.

Selling the practitioner's outcome to the budget-holder

Companies win the user with a 'do more, faster' story, then stall because the person who signs the contract is funding something else entirely, risk reduction, compliance, board-grade economics.

EnFiSub-pages ARE segmented (banks/private-credit/fintech) but all converge on velocity ('2x loans', '3x deals'), while the budget-holder (Chief Credit/Compliance Officer) funds RISK REDUCTION, not speed. Selling speed to a risk buyer.
ClairaRunning the hardest sale there is (category creation, 'no budget line', selling 'the future process not a product') while describing real proof (BC Partners, $5B firm) in efficiency/feature terms ('85% efficiency', 'agentic AI') rather than per-deal velocity/cost/risk dollars that create a budget owner.
EisenFounder's sharpest weapon is the dollar threat (fines+penalties+interest, 3-year fuse), but the site leads with 'AI on Autopilot' and sells one undifferentiated message to banks AND crypto (opposite regulatory urgency). Hiring 3 eng / 0 commercial.

Enterprise validation without an enterprise motion

The hardest gap to see from the inside. Marquee logos and a raise sized for upmarket, with no commercial engine, security/procurement motion, or named GTM hire to convert the halo into repeatable revenue.

Tabs$55M Series B raised to 'bring AI agents to the CFO's office' (enterprise, top-down) but the public footprint is a $2k/mo self-serve tier and a logo wall of seed/Series-A startups (Cursor, Statsig). 2 eng / 0 commercial hires.
AvantosSite reads unmistakably enterprise (SOC2, demo-only, Vanguard/SEI/Guardian/Mercer logos) and backers are large regulated institutions, yet zero open commercial roles and the founder still closes. Enterprise validation without an enterprise sales motion.

The distribution traps

Where the channel quietly works against the company: selling to your channel and around it at once, asking buyers to rip out entrenched systems with no 'why now', or a vision that attracts the wrong buyer.

CacheRuns two front doors for the same exchange fund: a self-serve direct-to-investor funnel ($100k min, refer-a-friend) AND a wholesale RIA channel ('your clients', advisor discounts). Cache is simultaneously the advisor's supplier and the advisor's bypass.
Irys InsurtechSells against entrenched legacy AMS (Applied Epic, AMS360), the highest-switching-cost rip-and-replace in insurance, but pitches a generic 'operating system' + borrowed analyst stats, with no broker-specific 'why move now', while sitting on an unused wedge (no-migration switching + 'you don't own your own data').
Lunos AIPR-driven '$100T agent-to-agent commerce' vision attracts CFOs and fintech press, but the shipped product (AR collections, success-priced 0.3% on collected) converts only a narrow operational buyer (AR analyst/controller who owns DSO). Demand wide, qualifiable buyer narrow.

You do not have to be right every time. You have to be interesting enough that a founder needs to understand why you think it.

CacheCapital Markets · wealth · confidence: High

The evidence: Runs two front doors for the same exchange fund: a self-serve direct-to-investor funnel ($100k min, refer-a-friend) AND a wholesale RIA channel ('your clients', advisor discounts). Cache is simultaneously the advisor's supplier and the advisor's bypass.

The hypothesis: Cache's next constraint is not founder-led selling but unresolved channel conflict between its direct-investor funnel and its RIA wholesale channel.

What would prove it wrong: If the direct-investor and RIA channels serve genuinely separate customers with no overlap, the conflict dissolves.

If I were the CEO of one of these companies

Seven questions I would put to my leadership team on Monday morning.

  • 1Are we hiring for the company we have, or the company we are trying to become?
  • 2Can anyone besides the founder consistently create and close pipeline?
  • 3If the founder stepped out for 60 days, would revenue keep growing?
  • 4Are customers buying the product, or are they buying the founder?
  • 5Which buyer segment actually converts most reliably, and are we resourcing that one, or all of them?
  • 6Does our pricing reflect buyer value, or internal guesswork?
  • 7Which assumption about our go-to-market is most likely wrong today?

If your honest answers to two or three of these are uncomfortable, that is not a reason to hire a CRO. It is a reason to get the engine right first.
